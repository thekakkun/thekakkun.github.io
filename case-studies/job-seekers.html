Job Seekers Study

April—June 2019

An American tech company tasked us with running the Japanese phase of a global study they were conducting to understand local job seekers and get feedback on their newly-released job aggregator.

Project Overview

Project Phases & Goals

  1. Desk Research

    2 weeks

    Bring clients up-to-date on Japanese job search trends and norms, in order to create a common baseline.

  4. User Interviews

    6 weeks

    Understand how Japanese job seekers are searching for jobs, and what their pain points are.

  7. Survey

    5 weeks

    Gather data on websites and search methods used by users as well as their satisfaction.

Overall Responsibilities

  • Project manager for local (Japanese) research
  • Main point of contact for client and outsourcing partners
  • Researcher for user interview phase, lead researcher for survey phase
  • Presentation of findings to client for all phases

Phase 1 – Desk Research

Process

  • Gather information on the types of employment available in Japan as well as past and current trends.
  • Create a report and present to the client.

My Responsibilities

  • Translation of presentation created by Japanese UX researcher
  • Presentation of report to clients

Challenges

There are large cultural diffences surrounding jobs when comparing Japan against the west. It was important that the client understood the Japanese job search norms so that they could correctly interpret finding from the interview and survey phase.

A mix of government data, personal anectodes, and examples of media depicting job search culture was used to make sure that the clients got a holistic view of job searching in Japan.

Some unique aspects of Japanese job search culture:

  • Simultaneous recruitment

    Large employers open new graduate rols on the same date. Universities and other 3rd parties organize career seminars in order to ensure that students are able to secure a position by the time they graduate.

  • Lifetime employment

    While attitudes are changing, many Japanese employers expected workers to stay at the company until retirement. Progression within the company is dependent not only on job performance, but loyalty to the company as well.

Key Takeaways

The contents of the presentation were used by the clients to inform what kinds of users they wanted to pursue for the interview and survey phase of the study.

After discussion, the clients selected the following user groups as targets for the following research phases:

  • New graduates
  • general job seekers
  • recent retirees

Phase 2 – User Interviews

Process

  • 90-minute one-on-one interviews with 12 participants (New graduates, general job seekers, recent retirees)
  • Interview session consisted of:
    • Learning about the participant's job search process (what services they used, what information they depended on, and any pain points they came across).
    • The final 30 minutes of the study was used to have the participant explore the client's job search tool, and gather feedback.

My Responsibilities

  • Note-taking in observation room.
  • Provide context and analysis for clients.
  • Presentation of results to the clients.

Key Takeaways

  • New graduates only use 2 select job search sites.
    They were satisfied with just using the sites which were endorsed by their colleges. They felt that they would become overwhelmed if they added any other job sites into their routine.

  • Recent retirees were frustrated at the jobs available to them.
    Many recent retirees were still looking to work as they wanted to make use of their time and contribute to society. However, they found that the jobs hiring seniors involved menial tasks, and the jobs they felt were more worthwhile went to younger applicants.

  • Participants found it difficult to use the client’s job search tool.
    While Japanese participants were used to initiating their search through filters, they client’s tool required participants to enter a keyword before getting results. In addition, many of the provided filters were not well localized for Japanese users.

Phase 3 – Survey

Methodology

  • Online survey with 300 samples.
  • Questions consisted of:
    • the services and tools used by the respondants as part of their job search.
    • satisfaction with the resources available.
    • whether they had seen or used the client's job search tool.

Challenges

The screening criteria and questions provided by the client were not well defined enough for me to create a translated Japanese survey that would be easy for respondents to complete.

  • I worked with clients to understand the data they wanted to gather from the survey, and localized the Japanese version so that the terminology used within the survey was in-line with the respondent’s mental models.
  • The screener and survey structure was overhauled in order to ensure that respondents did not need to answer unnecessary questions.

An example of the changes made to the survey questions

Client's design
  1. Which of the following best describes your current employment status?
    1. Permanent, full time
    2. Permanent, part time
    3. Retired
    4. etc.
  2. Are you currently searching for a job?
    1. I am not searching for jobs.
    2. I am currently searching for jobs.
      (set as "recent retirees" segment if 1c selected.)
Revised design
  1. Which of the following best describes your current employment status?
    1. Permanent, full time
    2. Permanent, part time
    3. Retired
    4. etc.
  2. (show if currently working)
    Will you be retiring (includes early retirement) within the next 12 months? Please select the answer that best matches your current status.
    1. Yes, I intend to retire within 12 months
    2. No, I do not intend to retire within 12 months
  3. (show if 2a selected)
    Are you planning to return to work? Please select the answer that best matches your current plans.
    1. Yes, I will be returning to work for my current employer after retirement
    2. Yes, I will be looking for a new job after retirement
      (set as "recent retirees" segment if selected.)
    3. No, I will NOT be looking for a new job after retirement
  4. (show if currently working, not intending to retire in the next 12 months)
    Are you currently searching for a job?
    1. I am not searching for jobs.
    2. I am currently searching for jobs.
      (set as "recent retirees" segment if 1c selected.)

Key Takeaways

  • The majority of respondents mentioned that they still used offline resources

    Many people still used offline resources as part of their current job search. Some frequently used resources included: free job advertisement magazines and the employment service centre run by the Japanese government.

  • About half of the respondents had never seen or used the client’s job search tool

    Of the participants that had experience with it, a majority were satisfied with it.

Outcome

After all research phases were completed, the clients held a workshop to summarize the findings from the study. I was invited to the workshop in order to provide additional context into the Japanese employment and job seeking experience.

The workshop concluded with us coming up with some “how might we?” questions that demanded further consideration.

  • As a job aggregator, how might we provide useful job filters dynamically?

    Japanese users expected very detailed job filters that include job fields and sub-fields, distance from train stations, etc.

  • How might we signal to users which jobs are willing to hire older workers while also considering age discrimination?

    Recent retirees were finding it difficult to find and get selected for meaningful roles post-retirement.

  • How might we provide value to students without further overwhelming them?

    Most new graduates only use two select job search sites and find information provided by them to be overwhelming.
